Policy on Data Protections

What is Personal Information

Personal information is any identifiable information. Some example of this would be:

  • Name
  • Address
  • Phone Number
  • Email Address
  • Social Security Number
  • Date of Birth
  • Biometric Data (such as fingerprints or facial recognition)
  • Medical Information
  • Financial Information (such as bank account numbers or credit card numbers)
  • Employment Information (such as job title and salary)
  • Location Data (such as GPS data)

Why Keep Personal Information Private

Keeping your personal information private protects against identity theft and digital/physical harm, maintains your privacy and autonomy. Personal information can be used to steal someone's identity, exploitation of the sensitive data leading to possible harm. Overall, it is your data that defines who you are, you have the right to make their own choice and the right to control how it is shared and used.

About GDPR

The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) is a comprehensive privacy law that was introduced by the EU in 2016. It is designed to give EU citizens greater control over their personal data and to unify data privacy regulations within the EU. The GDPR places strict requirements on organizations that collect, process, and store personal data. The GDPR aims to protect the privacy rights of EU citizens and to create a consistent set of data protection laws across the EU. The GDPR applies to all organizations that process the personal data of EU citizens, regardless of where the organization is located. Individuals have the right to request access to their personal data, to have their data corrected or deleted, and to object to the processing of their data.

Why was the GDPR created

The previous EU data protection directive was adopted in 1995, and it was considered outdated and inadequate in the face of new technological developments and global data flows. The GDPR was seen as a necessary update to ensure that individuals' privacy rights were protected in the digital age, and to provide businesses with clear and consistent rules for handling personal data across the EU.

The GDPR was created to modernize and harmonize data protection laws across the European Union (EU) and to give EU citizens more control over their personal data. It's designed to enhance cross-border cooperation among EU data protection authorities and to streamline regulatory compliance for businesses operating across the EU.

The Policy as Created

The GDPR places strict requirements on organizations that collect, process, and store personal data. The GDPR places limits on the transfer of personal data to countries outside the EU that do not provide adequate data protection. The GDPR gives individuals the right to object to the processing of their data for direct marketing purposes. The GDPR requires organizations to report data breaches to the relevant DPA within 72 hours of becoming aware of the breach. Failure to report a breach can result in significant fines. The Policy as Practiced

The GDPR's provisions are subject to interpretation, and different data protection authorities and courts may interpret them differently. This can lead to variations in how the regulation is enforced in different jurisdictions. The GDPR was written before many of the technologies that are now being used to collect and process personal data were developed. As a result, there may be gaps in the regulation's coverage of certain types of data processing activities. Not to mention the DPA have limited resources to enforce the GDPR effectively, which can result in uneven enforcement across different organizations and sectors. Each EU member state has its own national data protection laws, and these laws may influence how the GDPR is enforced in practice.

About the Social Movement

The privacy movement is a social and political movement that advocates for the protection of individuals' privacy rights, particularly in the digital age. After emerged in the 1960s and 1970s in response to concerns about government surveillance and the use of personal data by businesses, they have adapted a wide range of issues and concerns, including data privacy, surveillance, free speech, and consumer protection. The privacy movement faces ongoing challenges, including the emergence of new technologies like facial recognition and the increasing influence of tech companies in public life. They seek to promote transparency, accountability, and consent in the collection, use, and sharing of personal information.

Privacy Movement Goals

Raising awareness: One of the primary goals of the privacy movement is to educate people about the importance of privacy and the potential risks and consequences of data misuse or surveillance. Advocating for stronger privacy laws: The privacy movement seeks to influence policymakers and regulators to pass laws and regulations that protect individuals' privacy rights. This can involve lobbying for specific privacy legislation, providing input on proposed regulations, or engaging in legal challenges to privacy violations. Promoting best practices: The privacy movement encourages businesses, government agencies, and other organizations to adopt best practices for protecting individuals' privacy. This can include things like implementing privacy-by-design principles in product development, using encryption to secure data, or minimizing the collection and retention of personal information. Empowering individuals: The privacy movement seeks to empower individuals to take control of their own privacy by providing tools and resources for privacy self-defense. This can include using privacy-enhancing technologies like VPNs and encrypted messaging apps, adopting good password hygiene, and being vigilant about privacy risks when using social media or other online services. Engaging in public discourse: The privacy movement seeks to shape public discourse and debate around privacy issues by providing expert analysis and commentary, participating in public events and forums, and engaging with media outlets to raise awareness of privacy issues.

Examples in Other Countires

There is a growing demand for data privacy in China. Chinese citizens are becoming increasingly concerned about the collection and use of their personal data by the government and private companies. China has a sophisticated surveillance system that tracks citizens' behavior both online and offline, and that there are few legal protections for personal data privacy. There have been several incidents where personal data was misused or leaked, leading to public outrage and calls for stronger data privacy laws.

Examples in Other Countires

The United States does not have a comprehensive federal data protection law like the GDPR. Instead, there are a variety of sector-specific laws, such as the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) and the Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA), that apply to certain types of personal data. Data protection in the US tends to focus on responding to data breaches rather than preventing them. Laws such as the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) and the New York State Stop Hacks and Improve Electronic Data Security (SHIELD) Act require organizations to notify individuals and authorities of data breaches in a timely manner. In recent years, there has been a growing interest in data protection in the US, spurred in part by high-profile data breaches

Organizations are legal liable when they fail to implement adequate data protection measures that cause any damages resulting from data breaches, such as identity theft, financial losses, and reputational damage. There is some regulatory enforcement agencies such as the FTC that can take actions against organizations that violate data protection laws. One example, in 2019, the FTC fined Facebook $5 billion for violating a 2012 consent decree related to user privacy. Data breaches can result in the loss of business and damage to an organization's reputation, particularly if customers lose trust in the organization's ability to protect their personal information. High-profile data breaches can lead to negative media coverage and public backlash, which can damage an organization's brand and reputation. For each data breaches that occurs can lead to increased regulatory scrutiny and pressure for stronger data protection laws. This results in higher compliance costs and more regulatory burdens for affected organizations.
